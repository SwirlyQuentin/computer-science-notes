# **Long-term vs Short-term Scheduling**

Scheduling decisions are made at **two different levels** in an operating system.

---

## **[[Long-Term Scheduling]]**

Determines **which processes are allowed to enter the system and compete for the CPU**.

* Decides when a process should enter the **ready state**
* Moves processes into the [[Ready List]]
* Controls the **degree of multiprogramming** (how many processes are active)

## **Short-Term Scheduling**

Determines **which ready process actually runs on the CPU next**.

* Selects a process from the [[Ready List]]
* Assigns the CPU to that process
* Runs **much more frequently** than [[long-term scheduling]]

---

## **Walkthrough of Scheduling Flow**

1. Processes are created at unpredictable times and enter the system with the process state **new**.
2. **[[Long-term scheduling]]** decides which processes are moved to the [[Ready List]] to compete for the CPU.
3. Processes are also subject to [[long-term scheduling]] when [[Process Suspended State|suspended]] and later **reactivated by the OS**.
4. **[[Short-term scheduling]]** moves processes between the [[Ready List]] and the CPU.
5. When a running process becomes **blocked** and later reenters the [[Ready List|RL]], **short-term scheduling** is applied again so the process can compete for the CPU.

---

# **Preemptive vs Non-Preemptive Scheduling**

Scheduling algorithms fall into two major categories.

## **[[Non-Preemptive Scheduling]]**

A running process **keeps the CPU until it finishes or blocks**.

* The OS **does not interrupt** the running process
* The process continues running until:
  * It **terminates**
  * It **blocks on a resource**

## **[[Preemptive Scheduling]]**

The OS **may interrupt a running process** and assign the CPU to another process.

Preemption can occur when:

* A **new process enters the [[Ready List|RL]]**
* A **previously blocked or suspended process re-enters**
* The OS **periodically interrupts the running process** to give other processes a chance to run

---

# **Priority for Short-Term Scheduling**

Each process (or thread) has a **[[Process Priority|priority]] value**, which indicates its **importance relative to other processes**.

The **scheduler uses this value** to decide **which process should run next**.

If two or more processes have the same priority, the **[[arbitration rule]]** determines which process proceeds.

---

## **How Priority Can Be Assigned**

[[Process Priority|Priority]] may be:

* **Static** – Assigned once when the process is created
* **Dynamic** – Changes during execution based on system behavior

---

## **Parameters That Can Affect Priority**

### **Arrival**

The point in time when the process **enters the [[Ready List|RL]]**.

### **Departure**

The point in time when the process **leaves the [[Ready List|RL]]** by:

* Entering the **blocked state**
* Entering the **suspended state**
* **Terminating**

### **Attained CPU Time**

The amount of **CPU time already used** by the process since arrival.

### **Real Time in System**

The amount of **actual wall-clock time** the process has spent in the system since arrival.

### **Total CPU Time**

The total amount of CPU time the process will use between **arrival and departure**.

For [[short-term scheduling]] this is often called the **[[CPU burst]]**.

### **External Priority**

A **numeric priority assigned explicitly** when the process is created.

### **[[Deadline (Scheduling)|Deadline]]**

A **specific time by which the process must complete**.

### **Period**

The **time interval for periodically repeating computations**. The end of each period acts as a [[Deadline (Scheduling)|deadline]] for that computation.

### **Other Considerations**

Other system factors may influence [[Process Priority|priority]], such as:

* Memory usage
* Resource requirements
* Current system load

---

# **[[Process Priority|Priority]] for [[Long-Term Scheduling]]**

[[Long-term scheduling]] also uses **priorities**, based on **similar parameters** as [[short-term scheduling]].

However, there are some important differences:

* [[Long-term scheduling]] **occurs much less frequently**
* Decisions are made at a **larger time scale**
* It controls **which processes enter the system**

---

## **Different Meanings of Arrival and Departure**

In **[[long-term scheduling]]**, these terms refer to different points:

* **Arrival** – The time of **process creation**
* **Departure** – The time of **process destruction**

Because of this, **attained CPU time** and **total CPU time** may have **different meanings** compared to [[short-term scheduling]].
