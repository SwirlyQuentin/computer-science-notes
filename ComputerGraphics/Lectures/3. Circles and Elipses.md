## 1. Basic 2D Object Definitions

### Lines and Polylines
- **Line**: A straight segment between two points.
- **Polyline**: A sequence of connected line segments drawn between **ordered pairs of vertices**.
- **Closed Polyline**: When the first and last vertex connect, forming a **[[polygon]]**.
- **Simple [[Polygon]]**: A [[polygon]] whose edges **do not intersect**.

### Convex vs Concave Polygons

**[[Convex Polygon]]**
- A line drawn between **any two points inside the polygon** stays entirely inside the polygon.

**Concave Polygon**
- There exists **at least one pair of points** where the line between them passes **outside the polygon**.

### Special Polygons
Common examples:
- Square
- Rectangle
- Triangle

### Circles
A **circle** is the set of all points that are the same distance from a center point.

- The distance from the center to the edge is called the **radius** \( r \).

Equation of a circle centered at the origin:

$$
x^2 + y^2 = r^2
$$

Properties:
- All points satisfy the equation.
- Circles are often **approximated by polygons with many sides** in computer graphics.

### Ellipses
An **ellipse** can be thought of as a **scaled circle**.

- Scaling in the **x direction** stretches the circle horizontally.
- Scaling in the **y direction** stretches it vertically.

An **axis-aligned ellipse** has its axes aligned with the coordinate axes.

# 2. Representing Shapes

### [[Polygon]] Representation in Graphics
In graphics systems such as **OpenGL**, objects are constructed using:

- **Triangles**
- **Quads**

These primitives are used to:
- Build arbitrary polygons
- Approximate smooth surfaces

Triangles are commonly used because they are **always planar and easy for GPUs to render**.

---

### Vertex and Edge Tables

Objects can be stored using **vertex and edge tables**.

#### [[Vertex Table]]
- Each vertex is listed **once**.
- Stores the coordinates of points.

Example:

| Index | x | y |
|------|---|---|
| 0 | 1 | 2 |
| 1 | 3 | 5 |

#### [[Edge Table]]
- Each edge is an **ordered pair of indices** referring to the [[vertex table]].

Example:

| Edge | Vertex A | Vertex B |
|-----|-----------|-----------|
| 0 | 0 | 1 |

Properties:
- Efficient representation
- Useful for transformations
- Edges are typically stored in **counterclockwise order**

Counterclockwise ordering helps determine:
- Inside vs outside
- Polygon orientation
- Surface normals

# 3. Splines

## Representing General Curves

Straight line segments are often **not smooth enough** to represent curves.

Splines solve this by using **higher order polynomials**.

Advantages:
- Smooth curves
- Easy to compute
- Cheaper than computing functions like:
  - ($\sin$)
  - ($\cos$)
  - ($\log$)

---

## Uses of Splines

Splines are used for:

### Shape Representation
- Smooth outlines in **2D**
- Surface patches in **3D**

### Animation
- Motion paths
- Interpolating between **keyframes**

### Function Approximation
- Polynomials are cheaper to compute than many mathematical functions.

# 4. [[Hermite Curve|Hermite]] Curves

## Linear [[Interpolation]] (Polylines)

Polylines are **first-order polynomial interpolations** between points.

Given two points \(P\) and \(Q\):

$$
x(t) = (1-t)P + tQ
$$

where

$$
0 \le t \le 1
$$

- $(1-t)$ and $t$ are **weighting functions**.

When:
- \(t = 0\) → point = \(P\)
- \(t = 1\) → point = \(Q\)

---

## Higher Order [[Interpolation]]

Splines extend interpolation using **higher order polynomials**.

Benefits:
- Smoother curves
- Better approximations of natural shapes
- Control over curve slope

---

## [[Hermite Curve]] Representation

A **Hermite curve** is defined by:

- Two **[[control points]]**
  - \(P\) (start)
  - \(Q\) (end)

- Two **tangent vectors**
  - \(v\) (initial tangent)
  - \(w\) (final tangent)

These tangents control the **direction the curve enters and leaves each point**.

Multiple Hermite curves can be **chained together** to form longer smooth curves.

---

## Hermite Weighting

Hermite curves use **third-degree polynomial weighting functions**.

Key behavior:

### At \(t = 0\)
- The curve starts at **point \(P\)**
- Initial tangent vector determines the direction.

### At \(t = 1\)
- The curve ends at **point \(Q\)**
- Final tangent vector determines the ending direction.

# 5. [[Bezier Curve|Bezier]] Curves

[[Bezier Curve|Bezier]] curves are another [[spline]] representation.

Comparison:

| Hermite | Bezier |
|-------|-------|
| 2 points + 2 tangent vectors | 4 control points |
| Tangents explicitly defined | Tangents implied by control points |

Advantages:
- Easier to store
- Easy to edit
- Widely supported in graphics tools

Common uses:
- **Font rendering**
- **Vector graphics**
- **Design software**

# 6. Drawing Curves (Pseudocode)

## Drawing a Circle

The circumference of a circle runs from:

$$
\theta = 0 \text{ to } 2\pi
$$

Algorithm:

1. Start with center $(c_x, c_y)$
2. Choose radius $r$
3. Increment $\theta$ by small steps
4. Compute coordinates
5. Plot the point

Coordinates:

$$
x = r \cos(\theta)
$$

$$
y = r \sin(\theta)
$$

Final plotted point:

$$
(x + c_x,\; y + c_y)
$$

---

## Drawing an Ellipse

An ellipse has **two radii**:

- $r_x$ — horizontal radius  
- $r_y$ — vertical radius

Angle still ranges from:

$$
0 \le \theta \le 2\pi
$$

Coordinates:

$$
x = r_x \cos(\theta)
$$

$$
y = r_y \sin(\theta)
$$

Final plotted point:

$$
(x + c_x,\; y + c_y)
$$

Difference from circles:
- Circle → same radius in all directions
- Ellipse → different radii on each axis
