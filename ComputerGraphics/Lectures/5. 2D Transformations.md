# Geometric Transformations

## How Geometric Transformations Are Used

- At the lowest level, graphical objects are **collections of vertices**.
- Objects have:
  - **Location**
  - **Orientation**
  - **Size**

These correspond to the three fundamental transformations:

- **Translation** – changes position  
- **Rotation** – changes orientation  
- **Scaling** – changes size  

---

- **3D coordinate geometry**

# Vectors

Vectors are represented using **bold italic notation**.

Example: **v**

## Basis Vectors

Any vector in a plane can be defined as the **sum of two non-collinear basis vectors**.

A **basis for a vector space** is a set of vectors with these properties:

- The vectors are **linearly independent**
- Any vector **v** can be expressed as a **linear combination of the basis vectors**

## Scalar Multiplication

Multiplying a vector by a scalar:

- Changes the **magnitude**
- Keeps the **direction** (unless the scalar is negative)

---

# Linear Transformations

A **linear transformation** maps points **around the origin** to another point in the same coordinate system.

Properties:

- Result is another point **transformed about the origin**
- Includes:
  - **Scaling**
  - **Rotation**
- Does **not include translation**, because translation moves the origin.

---

# Transformations as Matrices

Linear transformations can be represented using matrices.

Let a transformation matrix be:

$$
T =
\begin{bmatrix}
a & b \\
c & d
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Let a vector be:

$$
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Then the transformation is:

$$
T
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
a & b \\
c & d
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 \\
x_2
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
ax_1 + bx_2 \\
cx_1 + dx_2
\end{bmatrix}
$$

---

# Standard Basis Vectors

Let the **standard basis vectors** be:

$$
e_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
0
\end{bmatrix}
\qquad
e_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

## Transforming \(e_1\)

$$
T
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
0
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
a \\
c
\end{bmatrix}
$$

## Transforming \(e_2\)

$$
T
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
b \\
d
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Therefore:

$$
T(e_1)=
\begin{bmatrix}
a \\
c
\end{bmatrix}
\qquad
T(e_2)=
\begin{bmatrix}
b \\
d
\end{bmatrix}
$$

This gives a **strategy for deriving transformation matrices**:

> A transformation matrix is constructed by placing the transformed basis vectors as its columns.

---

# Scaling

Scaling changes the size of an object.

Let:

$$
v =
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y
\end{bmatrix}
\qquad
v' =
\begin{bmatrix}
x' \\
y'
\end{bmatrix}
$$

- **v** – original vector  
- **v'** – transformed vector

The scaling transformation:

$$
v' = Sv
$$

## Scaling the Basis Vectors

Scale **x** by \(s_x\):

$$
e_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
0
\end{bmatrix}
\rightarrow
\begin{bmatrix}
s_x \\
0
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Scale **y** by \(s_y\):

$$
e_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
\rightarrow
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
s_y
\end{bmatrix}
$$

## Scaling Matrix

$$
S =
\begin{bmatrix}
s_x & 0 \\
0 & s_y
\end{bmatrix}
$$

## Example

Vector:

```
v = (2,1)
```

Scaled by:

```
S(3,2)
```

Result:

```
v' = (6,2)
```

### Properties

- **Non-uniform scaling** does **not preserve angles**
- **Uniform scaling** ( \(s_x = s_y\) ) **does preserve angles**

---

# Rotation

Rotation rotates a vector by an angle **θ** about the origin.

Transformation:

$$
v' = R_\theta v
$$

## Deriving the Rotation Matrix

To derive the rotation matrix, we determine how the **basis vectors** transform when rotated.

A rotation transformation rotates every vector by the same angle **$\theta$ around the origin**.

Because **any vector can be written as a linear combination of the basis vectors**, we only need to see how the basis vectors change.

The standard basis vectors are:

$$
e_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
0
\end{bmatrix}
\qquad
e_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

---

### Rotating $e_1 = (1,0)$

The vector $e_1$ lies on the **positive x-axis**.

If we rotate it **counterclockwise by $\theta$**, it moves to a point on the **unit circle**.

On the unit circle:

- $x = \cos\theta$
- $y = \sin\theta$

So the rotated vector becomes:

$$
e_1' =
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta \\
\sin\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

This becomes the **first column of the rotation matrix**.

---

### Rotating $e_2 = (0,1)$

The vector $e_2$ lies on the **positive y-axis**, which corresponds to an angle of:

$$
90^\circ = \frac{\pi}{2}
$$

After rotating by **$\theta$**, its new angle becomes:

$$
\frac{\pi}{2} + \theta
$$

Using trigonometric identities:

$$
\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2}+\theta\right) = -\sin\theta
$$

$$
\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2}+\theta\right) = \cos\theta
$$

So the rotated vector becomes:

$$
e_2' =
\begin{bmatrix}
-\sin\theta \\
\cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

This becomes the **second column of the rotation matrix**.


### First Column

$$
e_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
0
\end{bmatrix}
\rightarrow
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta \\
\sin\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

### Second Column

$$
e_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
\rightarrow
\begin{bmatrix}
-\sin\theta \\
\cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$


---

### Constructing the Rotation Matrix

Since the **columns of a transformation matrix are the transformed basis vectors**, we place them in the matrix:

$$
R_\theta =
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\
\sin\theta & \cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

## Rotation Matrix

$$
R_\theta =
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\
\sin\theta & \cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Applying the transformation:

$$
R_\theta
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
x\cos\theta - y\sin\theta \\
x\sin\theta + y\cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$

So:

```
x' = xcosθ − ysinθ
y' = xsinθ + ycosθ
```

### Properties of Rotation

- Preserves **lengths**
- Preserves **angles**

---

# Translation

Translation moves an object without changing its orientation.

Translation is **not a linear transformation**, because it **moves the origin**.

It cannot be represented using a **2×2 matrix**.

However, we can write:

$$
v' = v + t
$$

where

$$
t =
\begin{bmatrix}
dx \\
dy
\end{bmatrix}
$$

But vector addition is inconsistent with matrix transformation pipelines.

---

# Homogeneous Coordinates

To support translation using matrices, we use **homogeneous coordinates**.

This adds an extra dimension to vectors.

Translation matrix:

$$
T =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & dx \\
0 & 1 & dy \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Vector becomes:

$$
v =
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

Transformation:

$$
Tv =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & dx \\
0 & 1 & dy \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
x + dx \\
y + dy \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
$$

---

# Composition of Transformations

Complex transformations are created by **combining multiple transformations**.

Important rule:

> Transformations are applied **from right to left**.

Example:

```
TRSv
```

Execution order:

1. **Scale**
2. **Rotate**
3. **Translate**
